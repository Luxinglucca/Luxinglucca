# Trinity Community Food Pantry: Funding a Snack Bag Program

Student consulting project, University of Illinois (Gies BA 301), Spring 2026. I led a six-student team helping a food pantry in Jacksonville, FL figure out how to fund and launch "Snack Bags," a take-home food program for local schoolkids.

**The short version:** the pantry wanted to feed kids but had one donor pool and no fundraising system. We built a snack bag plan that hit $2.00 per bag (half the client's cost target), sized the actual need at a partner high school, and left behind a fundraising playbook designed so high school volunteers could run it after we were gone.

## The problem

Trinity Community Food Pantry runs on donations from a single church congregation, and the church itself was operating at a deficit. The pantry director wanted to launch a snack bag program for schoolchildren. In North Florida, 1 in 5 children face food insecurity. At the partner high school, 848 of 1,638 enrolled students qualify for free or reduced lunch, and staff estimated 100 to 200 students attend school primarily to get a meal.

The real problem wasn't the bags. It was that the pantry had no repeatable way to raise money, so any new program would stall the moment it launched. Our scope shifted mid-project once we understood that, and the final recommendation was a fundraising system, not just a menu.

## My role

I was the team leader and ran the client relationship: scoping calls, agendas, and the midpoint and final presentations. The fundraising guidebook was my idea and my main workstream. I also did donor prospect research alongside two teammates, using GuideStar and Nexis Uni to find foundations and companies with a history of funding hunger relief in the Jacksonville area. I helped on the snack bag model but the credit for that build belongs to my teammates.

## What I'd do differently

Three things, honestly:

1. **Get real data first.** We spent early weeks researching food options when the most valuable input was student survey data. Getting surveys distributed depended on a school counselor connection that took longer than expected, and we never got the response volume we wanted. Next time I'd push on data access in week one, before building anything.
2. **Narrow the scope sooner.** If you compare our midpoint and final presentations, the problem statement changes. We carried two parallel workstreams (donors and snack bags) longer than we should have before committing to the fundraising system as the core recommendation.
3. **Test the handoff.** The guidebook was designed for high school students to run, but the semester ended before we could watch anyone actually use it. I still don't know how the handoff went, and I'd have liked to stay available for even a couple of Zoom calls to walk the first volunteers through it. Designing for handoff isn't the same as proving the handoff works.
